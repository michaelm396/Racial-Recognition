Skin Vision



We live in the age of the algorithm. Increasingly, the decisions that affect our lives where we go to school, how much we pay for health insurance, whether we get approved for a car loan are all being made not by humans, but by mathematical models. In theory, this should lead to greater fairness. Everyone is judged according to the same rules, and bias is eliminated, yet there are consistent inconsistencies in these often unregulated models. Across the globe, we continue to adopt widely used computer systems utilizing these mathematical models without first evaluating its inclusive nature.

In particular, Facial Recognition softwares tend to assume what a face should resemble based on spatial features, gradient shadows, and other unique characteristics using computational algorithms. This could result in specific human-passing characteristics such as white skin tones being prioritized in algorithms over darker skin tones because developers assume a bases for white skin as a depiction for all people. When facial recognition systems assume that people are uniquely the same, then the system could misidentify those not fitting within the scope of the ideal face space as faceless, invisible, or nonexistent.

NEVI

Nevi encodes a persons identity independent of pose and illumination. It evaluates small-scale variations of skin pigmentation. The novel skin segmentation is based on gray scale texture analysis that was developed to perform outlier detection in the face. Most like other FR systems, it does not require color input.
